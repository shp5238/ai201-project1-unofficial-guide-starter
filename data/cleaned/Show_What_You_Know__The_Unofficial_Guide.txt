Show What You Know:
The Unofficial Guide
Colleges have two kinds of knowledge: the official kind (course catalogs, housing
handbooks, university websites) and the real kind тАФ the stuff students actually share with
each other to survive. Rate My Professor reviews. Subreddit threads about which dining
hall is worth the walk. Anonymous posts about which off-campus apartments have mold
problems. Discord servers where seniors tell freshmen what professors actually want on
exams.
In this project, you'll build The Unofficial Guide: a RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation)
system that makes this kind of student-generated knowledge searchable and answerable.
A user asks a plain-language question тАФ "Is the housing lottery actually random?" or
"Which CS professor gives the most useful feedback?" тАФ and gets a grounded, cited
answer drawn from real documents you collected.
This is your first production AI project. More structure is provided here than in later projects
тАФ use it to build the habits (spec first, evaluate honestly, document completely) that you'll
need when that structure is gone.
ЁЯОп Goals
By completing this project, you will be able to:
тЧПтАЛ Build an end-to-end document processing pipeline: ingestion, chunking, and
embedding.
тЧПтАЛ Set up and query a vector store for semantic search.
тЧПтАЛ Generate grounded responses using retrieved context.
тЧПтАЛ Design and run an evaluation framework to measure how well your system actually
works.
тЧПтАЛ Document your design decisions so someone else could understand and extend
your system.

тЬЕ Features
Required Features
тАЛ
Document Ingestion Pipeline: Collect and process at least 10 documents from
your chosen domain. Your pipeline must: load the raw documents, clean or
preprocess them as needed (remove navigation text, ads, etc.), and produce
structured text ready for chunking. Describe this process in your README.
тАЛ
Chunking Strategy: Split your documents into chunks using a deliberate strategy
тАФ not just "split every 500 characters." Your planning.md must explain your chunk
size, overlap, and why those choices fit your documents. For example, review-style
text may warrant smaller chunks than long-form guides.
тАЛ
Vector Store and Semantic Search: Embed your chunks and store them in a
vector database. Given a user query, retrieve the top relevant chunks using
semantic similarity search. Your README should name the embedding model you
used and reflect on what tradeoffs you'd consider if you were choosing for a
production system (cost, context length, multilingual support, local vs. API).
тАЛ
Grounded Response Generation: Use an LLM to generate an answer to the user's
query using only the retrieved chunks as context. Responses should not rely on the
model's general knowledge тАФ they should be grounded in what was retrieved.
Include source attribution (which document(s) the answer draws from) in every
response.
тАЛ
Query Interface: Build a basic interface for querying your system. This can be a
simple web UI, a command-line tool, or a notebook тАФ but it must be usable enough
to demonstrate in your video without explaining how to navigate it.
тАЛ
Evaluation Report: Design 5 test questions with ground-truth answers, then run
your system on each and evaluate the results. For each question, your report should
document: the question, the correct answer, what your system returned, which
chunks were retrieved, and whether the retrieval and response were accurate,
partially accurate, or inaccurate. Identify at least one failure case and explain why it
happened.
Stretch Features
Complete any of these for extra credit. Update your planning.md before starting each one.
тАЛ
Hybrid Search: Combine semantic search with keyword (BM25) search and
compare results to semantic-only.

тАЛ
Chunking Strategy Comparison: Test 2+ chunking approaches on the same query
set and report which performed better and why.
тАЛ
Metadata Filtering: Allow users to filter by document source, date, or rating (e.g.,
only show reviews from the past year).
тАЛ
Conversational Memory: Support multi-turn queries where the system remembers
context from the previous question.

тЧПтАЛ Test retrieval before you add generation. A lot of RAG failures are retrieval failures тАФ

Milestone 3: Build the Document Pipeline
тП░ ~2тАУ3 hours
Your pipeline has two jobs: load your documents into memory and split them into chunks
your embedding model can work with. Most RAG failures trace back to bad chunks тАФ
chunks that are too large dilute the relevant information, chunks that are too small lose
context. Build this carefully and verify the output before moving on. Don't skip the chunk
inspection step.
Start simple. If you haven't collected your documents yet, begin with plain .txt files rather
than live web scraping. Some sources (like Rate My Professors) are difficult to scrape due
to JavaScript rendering or blocked requests тАФ you may need to copy text manually, use
PDFs, or export to a structured format. This is normal and expected. Useful AI prompts if
you hit scraping issues: "What are different ways to extract text from a JavaScript-rendered
website?" or "How can I convert unstructured text from a forum thread into a plain text file
for processing?"
The 2тАУ3 hour estimate reflects a careful, incremental process тАФ loading, cleaning,
chunking, and validating at each step. If you find yourself finishing in 20 minutes by having
AI generate everything at once, you're moving too fast. Come back and verify each stage
before relying on it in the next.
тАЛ
Use your planning.md as a prompt to an AI tool (Claude, Copilot, ChatGPT) to
generate your ingestion and chunking code. Share your Documents section (what
file types and sources you have), your Chunking Strategy section, and your pipeline
diagram. Ask the AI to implement a script that loads your documents, cleans them,
and produces chunks matching your specified chunk size and overlap. Review what
it generates: does it match your spec? Does it handle the document structure you
described? Correct anything that doesn't fit, and ask the AI to explain any part you
don't understand.
тАЛ
Write a script that loads all your documents. If you're scraping from URLs, collect
the raw text. If you're using local files, load them from disk. Save the raw text to a
consistent format before you start cleaning.

your system to use:тАЛ

that appears on every page.тАЛ
Keep: The actual review text, opinions, ratings, descriptions, and any context
needed to understand the content (e.g., the professor's name or course number in a
review).тАЛ

update planning.md to reflect why.

Common issues and how to diagnose them:
тЧПтАЛ Empty chunks: Your splitter is producing zero-length strings тАФ add a len(chunk)

тЧПтАЛ All chunks the same length: Your chunker is splitting mechanically without
respecting content boundaries тАФ consider whether paragraph or sentence splitting
fits your documents better.
тЧПтАЛ Chunks from the wrong document: Check that your metadata (source filename) is
attached correctly to each chunk.

Milestone 4: Embed Your Chunks and Test
Retrieval
тП░ ~1-2 hours
Embed your chunks and load them into a vector store, then test retrieval before you layer
on generation. This step is where most retrieval bugs surface тАФ and they're far easier to
debug here than after you've wired in an LLM. Don't move to Milestone 5 until retrieval is
returning relevant results.
тАЛ
Use your planning.md Retrieval Approach section and your pipeline diagram to
prompt an AI tool to generate your embedding and retrieval code. Give it your
diagram to establish the full architecture, then ask it to implement the embedding
step (loading chunks from your ingestion pipeline, embedding with
all-MiniLM-L6-v2, storing in ChromaDB with source metadata) and a retrieval

function. If the generated code uses a ChromaDB API call or pattern you don't
recognize, ask the AI to explain it тАФ understanding what the code does is part of
the exercise.
тАЛ
Set up your embedding model. The recommended default is all-MiniLM-L6-v2
from sentence-transformers тАФ it runs locally with no API key and no rate limits.
Load it with SentenceTransformer("all-MiniLM-L6-v2").
тАЛ
Embed all your chunks and load them into ChromaDB (or your chosen vector store)
along with metadata for each chunk: at minimum, the source document name and
the chunk's position in that document. You'll need source metadata later for
attribution.
тАЛ
Write a retrieval function that accepts a query string and returns the top-k most
relevant chunks along with their source information. Start with k=4 or k=5. If you
retrieve too few chunks, the relevant content may not be in the set at all. If you
retrieve too many, you dilute the context with loosely related material that can pull
the LLM's response off-target. You'll tune this after you've seen real results.
тАЛ
Test retrieval with at least 3 of your 5 evaluation plan queries. For each, print the
returned chunks and their distance scores. Ask: are these actually relevant to the
question?тАЛ
Good retrieval тАФ specific, on-topic, from the right source:тАЛ
Query: "What do students say about Professor Smith's exams?" Top result:
"Professor Smith's midterms are heavily curved and focus on lecture slides. Multiple
reviewers mentioned that attendance matters more than the textbook." (distance:
0.18)тАЛ
Bad retrieval тАФ wrong topic, or right topic but from the wrong source:тАЛ
Top result: "The parking situation near the CS building has gotten worse since
construction started." (distance: 0.61)High distance score + off-topic content =
retrieval failure, probably caused by chunks that are too small to carry enough
semantic signal.
тАЛ
If retrieval is returning chunks that seem unrelated, debug before moving on:
тЧЛтАЛ Print a retrieved chunk in full тАФ does it actually contain relevant content,
or does it just have a few words in common with your query?
тЧЛтАЛ Check distance scores тАФ scores above 0.6тАУ0.7 indicate weak matches. If
your best result has a high score, your chunks may be too short or too noisy.
тЧЛтАЛ Check chunk content тАФ if chunks look like fragments or HTML leftovers,
the cleaning/chunking stage didn't finish correctly.
тЧЛтАЛ Check metadata тАФ if results are coming from the wrong source, verify that
each chunk was stored with the correct source filename in its metadata.
тЧЛтАЛ Adjust chunk size тАФ if retrieval consistently pulls loosely related content,
try larger chunks that carry more semantic context per embedding.

Milestone 5: Wire Up Generation and Build Your
Interface
тП░ ~1-2 hours
Connect retrieval to an LLM to generate grounded answers, then build a usable interface.
The key engineering challenge here is grounding: your prompt must instruct the LLM to
answer from the retrieved context only тАФ not from its general training knowledge. Without
this, your system will produce confident-sounding answers that have nothing to do with
your documents.
тАЛ
Use your planning.md and pipeline diagram to prompt an AI tool to generate the
generation and interface code. Your prompt should include: your grounding
requirement (answers from retrieved context only, with source attribution), the
output format you want (answer + source list), and the Gradio skeleton structure if
you're using it. Ask the AI to wire it all together. Before running the generated code,
read through it тАФ make sure the system prompt actually enforces grounding, not
just suggests it, and that source attribution is programmatically guaranteed rather
than left to the LLM to add on its own.
тАЛ
Connect to your LLM. The recommended default is Groq's
llama-3.3-70b-versatile, which is free-tier and OpenAI-compatible тАФ initialize it
with from groq import Groq and your GROQ_API_KEY from .env. Write a prompt
template that passes the retrieved chunks as context and explicitly instructs the

model to answer only from that context. Example: "Answer the question using only
the information in the provided documents. If the documents don't contain enough
information to answer, say 'I don't have enough information on that.'"
тАЛ
Add source attribution to your response format. The LLM's response should name
which document(s) the answer came from тАФ either by instructing the model to cite
sources in its response, or by appending retrieved source names programmatically
after generation.
тАЛ
Test grounded generation end-to-end on 2тАУ3 queries. The test: could this response
have come from anywhere other than your retrieved chunks? If yes, it's a grounding
failure тАФ even if the answer happens to be correct.тАЛ
Grounded response тАФ answer traceable to retrieved text, source cited:тАЛ
"According to student reviews of Professor Smith (source: rmp_smith_reviews.txt),
exams are heavily curved and focus on lecture material rather than the textbook.
Several reviewers specifically recommend attending every class."тАЛ
Non-grounded response тАФ draws on LLM training knowledge, no citation:тАЛ
"Professor Smith likely structures exams similarly to most CS professors,
emphasizing core concepts and problem-solving skills. It's generally a good idea to
review lecture notes and practice past exams for any upper-division course."тАЛ
The second response sounds authoritative and may even be correct тАФ but it came
from the model's training data, not your documents. If your system returns this kind
of response, your grounding instruction needs tightening.тАЛ
Also ask a question your documents don't cover. The system should explicitly say it
doesn't have enough information тАФ not generate a plausible-sounding answer from
general knowledge.
тАЛ
Build your query interface. The recommended approach is a Gradio web UI тАФ add
gradio>=6.9.0 to your requirements.txt, then pip install gradio. A minimal
working interface looks like this:

sources = "\n".join(f"тАв {s}" for s in result["sources"])

with gr.Blocks() as demo:

inp.submit(handle_query, inputs=inp, outputs=[answer, sources])

demo.launch()
